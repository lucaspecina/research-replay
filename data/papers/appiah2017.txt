Title: The Effect of Education Expenditure on Per Capita GDP in Developing Countries

Authors: Elizabeth N. Appiah
Affiliation: Department of Economics, Marketing and Services, Faculty of Business Administration, Pentecost University College, Accra, Ghana

Journal: International Journal of Economics and Finance, Vol. 9, No. 10, 2017
DOI: 10.5539/ijef.v9n10p136
License: Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 (Open Access)

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Is further public and private investment in education warranted in developing countries that is economically efficient? Does an increase in education expenditure generate a positive impact on per capita GDP in developing countries? If so, is the impact different from that of Sub-Saharan African (SSA) countries? Education is one of the key factors of promoting economic growth because of its role in enhancing human capital thus productivity. However, adverse macroeconomic conditions and increased competition for scarce public funds have reduced governments' capacity to expand education expenditure to improve labor productivity. The author uses the 'system' GMM estimator to estimate the effect of an increase in education expenditure on per capita GDP. The uniqueness of this paper is that unlike other studies where only one country or region is considered, this paper examines the impact of increased spending on education on per capita GDP in developing countries. The findings indicate that expansion in education expenditure in developing countries affects per capita GDP positively, and the effect is not different from that of SSA countries.

Keywords: education expenditure, gross enrollment rate, per capita GDP, human capital

================================================================================
DATA AND VARIABLES
================================================================================

Panel data: 139 developing countries over 1975-2015
Source: World Development Indicators (WDI) database, World Bank

Dependent variable:
  - Log of per capita GDP (in constant 2010 US$)

Variable of interest:
  - Education expenditure (edx): total government expenditure on education (% of government expenditure)

Control variables:
  - Labor force (lft): proportion of population ages 15+ that is economically active
  - Labor force squared (lft2): quadratic term for diminishing returns hypothesis
  - Gross primary enrollment (ger1): total pupils enrolled at primary level (public + private)
  - Gross secondary enrollment (ger2): total pupils enrolled at secondary level (public + private)
  - Exports: annual % growth of goods and services (constant 2010 US$)
  - SSA dummy (ssa): 1 if country is in Sub-Saharan Africa, 0 otherwise

Number of observations: 406
Number of countries: 139 (91 developing excl. SSA, 48 SSA)

================================================================================
METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

Theoretical framework:
  Neoclassical production function: Y = AF(K, L)
  Extended to: Y = f(edx_it, lft_it, K_it)
  where K is a vector of control variables (ger1, ger2, exports)

Estimation equation (in first differences for GMM):
  DeltaLgdppc_it = alpha*DeltaLgdppc_{i,t-1}
                   + beta1*Delta_edx_it
                   + beta2*Delta_lft_it
                   + beta3*Delta_lft2_it
                   + beta4*Delta_ger1_it
                   + beta5*Delta_ger2_it
                   + beta6*Delta_export_it
                   + beta7*Delta_ssa
                   + Delta_epsilon_it

Estimator: Two-step system GMM (Blundell and Bond, 1998)
  - Chosen over difference GMM for better finite sample bias and RMSE properties
  - Handles endogeneity between education expenditure and per capita GDP
  - Uses additional moment conditions to alleviate weak instruments problem
  - Asymptotically efficient and robust to heteroskedasticity
  - First differencing removes fixed country-specific effects

Endogeneity: Education expenditure and per capita GDP are assumed endogenous (bidirectional causality: more education spending -> more human capital -> higher GDP, and higher GDP -> more resources for education)

Two models estimated:
  (1) Without SSA interaction term
  (2) With SSA dummy variable

================================================================================
KEY RESULTS AND INTERPRETATION
================================================================================

1. EDUCATION EXPENDITURE (edx):
   - Coefficient: 0.585*** (all developing), 0.459*** (with SSA dummy)
   - Positive and highly significant: increased government spending on education
     raises per capita GDP in developing countries
   - The effect is slightly lower when controlling for SSA membership

2. LABOR FORCE / HUMAN CAPITAL (lft and lft2):
   - Linear term (lft): 11.351*** (positive) -- labor force participation increases GDP
   - Quadratic term (lft2): -0.056*** (negative) -- diminishing returns
   - This confirms Schultz (1999) prediction: marginal effect of human capital
     diminishes as education expenditure increases

3. MARGINAL EFFECT CALCULATIONS:
   For all developing countries (mean lft = 63.649):
     dLgdppc/dlft = 11.351 + 2*(-0.056)*63.649
                  = 11.351 - 7.129
                  = 4.222 > 0
     --> Net marginal effect is positive

   For SSA countries (mean lft = 74.473):
     dLgdppc/dlft = 10.967 + 2*(-0.057)*74.473
                  = 10.967 - 8.490
                  = 2.477 > 0
     --> Net marginal effect is positive but smaller than all-developing average

4. PRIMARY EDUCATION (ger1):
   - Coefficient: -1.059*** (negative and significant)
   - Primary education alone is NOT sufficient to improve per capita GDP
   - Most developing countries have already achieved universal primary education,
     so marginal returns to further primary enrollment are negative

5. SECONDARY EDUCATION (ger2):
   - Coefficient: 0.946*** (positive and significant)
   - Secondary education HAS a positive effect on per capita GDP
   - Fewer countries have reached universal secondary education, so marginal
     returns remain positive

6. EXPORTS:
   - Coefficient: 6.390*** (positive and significant)
   - Export growth has a favorable effect on per capita GDP
   - Improved labor productivity from education contributes to export capacity

7. SSA DUMMY:
   - Coefficient: -21.994 (p=0.743, NOT significant)
   - No statistically significant difference in the education-GDP relationship
     between SSA and other developing countries
   - However, the magnitude of the marginal effect is lower for SSA (2.477 vs 4.222)
   - This gap is attributed to lower enrollment rates and education expenditure in SSA

8. CONVERGENCE (L.Lgdppc):
   - Coefficient: 0.9548*** (close to 1.0)
   - Near-unit-root persistence in per capita GDP, indicating slow convergence

================================================================================
DISCUSSION
================================================================================

- Investment in education affects per capita GDP positively for all developing
  countries. The marginal effect when considering all developing countries (4.222)
  is higher than for SSA alone (2.477).

- Results are robust when controlling for endogeneity of education investment and
  per capita GDP, and controlling for labor force participation and exports.

- The negative coefficient on primary enrollment suggests that merely expanding
  primary education is insufficient -- quality and secondary education matter more
  for GDP growth in countries that have already achieved near-universal primary.

- SSA countries have higher labor force participation but lack the human capital
  skills needed to enhance productivity, explaining their lower per capita GDP
  despite higher export growth (driven by raw materials/natural resources exported
  without value addition).

- Consistent with: Ali & Jabeen (2015), Krueger & Lindahl (2001), Appiah &
  McMahon (2002), Petrakis & Stamatakis (2002), Hanushek & Kimko (2000),
  Bils & Klenow (2000).

================================================================================
CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

1. Expansion of education expenditure has a POSITIVE effect on per capita GDP
   in developing countries.

2. There is NO significant difference in the impact of education expenditure on
   per capita GDP between developing countries overall and SSA countries
   specifically (SSA dummy insignificant at p=0.743).

3. However, the MAGNITUDE of the marginal effect is higher in developing
   countries overall (4.222) than in SSA (2.477), attributable to lower
   enrollment rates and education spending in SSA.

4. Primary education alone is insufficient for GDP growth; secondary education
   has significant positive effects.

5. It is not just enrollment that matters for economic growth, but the quality
   of knowledge acquired through education (Hanushek & Woessmann, 2010).

6. Policy implication: governments in developing countries should prioritize
   education expenditure as a growth engine, with emphasis on secondary education
   quality and sustained investment over time.

================================================================================
METHODOLOGICAL NOTES FOR REPLICATION
================================================================================

- Data source: World Development Indicators (World Bank), 1975-2015
- 139 developing countries, panel data, 406 observations
- Estimator: Two-step system GMM (Blundell & Bond, 1998)
- Arellano-Bond test for serial correlation: null of no serial correlation not rejected
- Education expenditure and per capita GDP treated as endogenous
- All other regressors treated as exogenous: E[K_ij * epsilon_it] = 0
- Lagged dependent variable included to test convergence hypothesis
- Quadratic labor force term included to test diminishing returns
- First differencing applied to remove country fixed effects
